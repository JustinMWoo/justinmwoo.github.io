Minimally Invasive


Overview

Minimally Invasive is a VR experience where the player is thrust into the role of a surgeon in a futuristic dystopian universe where they must perform surgery on aliens and cyborgs. The player pilots a bus fitted with technology to shrink down and enter the patient and perform the necessary surgeries. After flying to the destination within the bus, the player can exit and use tentacle like mechanical arms to interact with environmental puzzles to treat the patient.

Tool Arms Overview

I designed the mechanical tool arms to mimic the controls of real world surgical tools if they were controlled from a first-person perspective while also keeping them fun to use. The arms can be deployed whenever the player is grounded. They are controlled by holding down the clutch button and moving and orienting the controller. The tool arms are physics based, allowing them to knock around objects in the environment and react to other forces.

This page will go through a quick demonstration of how each tool functions in game, followed by a more in-depth look on into the blueprints.

Tool Arm Movement

The transform of the head of the tool is set through the animation blueprint using inverse kinematics to orient the bones in the arm. Each arm has a target location that is moved directly as the player controls the tool. The target location is attached through a physics constraint to the collision parent (a set of collision objects that represent the collision of the tool head) in order to give the tool its physics-based movement. The control of the arm functions by taking a snapshot of the location and rotation of the player's hands relative to their character when the clutch button is pressed. Then the offset is calculated between the new transform of their hands and the snapshot. The location offset is applied to the target location to adjust its position. The rotation of the hand is adjusted to the orientation of the player and then transformed to the direction of the target location to rotate the tool in a way that feels natural to the player. Lastly, if the movement of the player's hand was great enough to cause the arm to move out of its maximum range, an adjusted offset vector is calculated by determining the length of a vector in the direction of the offset vector that intersects with the maximum radius of the arm.

The Tool Heads

A diverse array of tool heads are available for the player to use according to the task at hand. When the arm is deployed, a menu can be brought up to switch between the six tools at the player's disposal.

The Scalpel

The scalpel, unsurprisingly, is used for cutting. The cover can be removed by holding the trigger to unsheathe the blade and enable it to cut. When deployed, the tool shows which parts of the environment can be cut and which angle and location they must be cut at. The scalpel is used in conjunction with other tools in order to perform tasks including severing veins and redirecting blood flow. Physics constraints are leveraged to lock the scalpel into a plane of movement that lines up with the blade as it makes contact. These constraints also provide resistance to simulate more realistic cutting through tissue. Additionally, the scalpel can be used to fend off enemies.

The Syringe

This tool is used to siphon liquids from the environment to use throughout the body to perform different tasks. Liquids including acids and naturally occurring antibiotics and anti-inflammatories can be found throughout the body and, when siphoned into the syringe, will be stored in canisters located on the arms. These canisters can be removed and stored in the player's inventory. The tool can then be used to inject these fluids into tissues that require them. Through a clever use of physics constraints and collision manipulation, the needle of the syringe can only be inserted into tissues using the tip and will lock itself into movement in the direction of the needle when inserted while providing resistance to simulate insertion into tissue.

The Grasper

Used to extend the player's reach and provide increased strength, the graspers act simply to pick up and manipulate objects at a distance. The player may also encounter objects that are too large and heavy for the player to pick up with their hands that must be lifted using the graspers. The graspers utilize colliders at the tips of each of the grasper's claw arms to detect when an object is grabbed alongside physics constraints to simulate it being picked up and manipulated.

The Drill

This tool is used to break apart certain brittle structures in the body and can be used to uncover paths or resources hidden beneath them. By using physics fields and strain, the drill is able to leverage Unreal's chaos destruction to cause certain environmental features to shatter. Additionally, the drill allows the player to dynamically adjust the strength and size of the field to adjust the strength of the drill for tougher materials at the trade-off of precision or vice versa. The drill can also be used to defend the player.

The Scanner

The scanner is a diagnostic tool used to probe the environment and gather information about the player's surroundings. By default, it reveals a circular area at the location it is pointed at and reveals hidden patterns underneath the surface of the tissue. This can be used to solve puzzles and find connected elements throughout the patient's body. The tool utilizes sphere traces, overlay materials and material parameters to dynamically set the location in the world that should have overlay materials shown by the scanner.
The scanner also has a second function. By holding the trigger, the player is able to display a card describing the properties of the scanned object and how to interact with it.

The Laser

A multipurpose tool that is primarily used to provide a powerful laser that can cauterize bleeding tissues in the body after cutting them with the scalpel. This tool uses a line trace to cauterize spline meshes that line an adjustable spline. Once a percentage of these spline meshes are cauterized, the line itself will be considered complete and will automatically cauterize the remaining segments. The other use of the laser is to deal with distant threats with precision.

The Details: Tool Movement

If you made it through the demonstrations and would like a more in-depth look into how it works, you've come to the right section! The image above shows the main parts of the blueprint that control the movement of the mechanical tool arms. The mechanical arm hierarchy has the primarily visual skeletal mesh "ArmMesh" along with the "MovementCollisionParent", "TargetAnchor" and "TargetLocation". The movement collision parent allows for each of the mechanical arm tool heads to have a separate collision shape that can be changed during the game (a more detailed description of the tool structure is in the following section). The movement collision parent has physics simulated for it and is attached to the "TargetLocation" using physics constraints. The movement of the arm is then calculated using the "TargetAnchor" which is updated when the arm's clutch is released and the direction of the player's hand movement in relation to its snapshot location. Then a calculation determines if the target location is within the max range of the arm and sets the "TargetLocation" if it is. If it is not in range, there is a following calculation using the location out of range, the base of the arm and the "TargetAnchor" to determine a new location in the same direction as the intended movement that is within the maximum range.

The Details: Tool Heads

When the blueprint structure for the tool heads were designed, I wanted to make sure that we could easily swap the player's tools depending on the scenario and potentially add more tools to their arsenal as we came across more ideas. Thus, I created a system with a base Actor Component with basic functionality such as activating, deactivating and constructing the tools which could then also be overwritten in the child classes for each individual tool type. This allowed for the use of an array of each of the tool classes that were wanted on the player that would be initialized with a simple loop and could be changed at any time. It also meant that creating new tool arms was a simple process of creating a new child class, assigning some variables and adding the arm's functionality to the component.

The Details: Tool Inventory

For quick switching between the tool heads I created a simple inventory UI for them. The "WBP_MechArmsInventory" consists of several "WBP_MechArmButtons". These buttons contain a background image, thumbnail for the tool, the tool's name and a button with 3 blank images to accommodate the hexagonal shape of the buttons. When the buttons are clicked the index of the specific tool on the mech arm is determined and the tool on the arm is set. The buttons are initialized from the inventory by assigning each tool head to a button using a map to find the corresponding information for the tool.

The Details: Audio and WWise

For setting up audio I worked directly with our audio designer in order to create a system that would function how he envisioned it and would be easy for him to interact and test sounds in with minimal Unreal experience. There were many sounds systems that we worked on and created together, with one of the main ones being a switch for the sound based on the material that was collided with or cut. Using the system he designed within WWise for the audio mixing, I created several functions that changed the switches and variables on WWise's end to adjust the sounds accordingly and could be easily modified as new sounds were added. This was one of many examples where we collaborated together throughout the development of Minimally Invasive to design the sound system.
